Teaching Philosophy

About This Document

This text is the pedagogical framework and blueprint for the AI Agent Programming course. It outlines a project-centric curriculum designed for the inherent ambiguity of artificial intelligence evaluation, shifting the student mindset toward iterative production using a fail-first approach.

Each decision is accompanied by a “why” (the reason for that pedagogical decision).

What Is Taught

Course Objectives

  • Be able to plan, program, measure, test, and iteratively refine Agentic AI solutions.
  • Know which problems are best solved with agentic AI as opposed to other methods.
  • Develop personal guidelines for ethical use of LLMs and agentic AI.
  • Communicate AI competence using current industry language to both technical and non-technical audiences.
  • Grow an ability to self-learn in an ever-changing AI landscape.

How It Is Taught

This is a project-based curriculum that follows the AI agent development lifecycle of “build, test, ship, observe, refine, repeat.” Its structure is additionally positioned to challenge the student’s assumptions with a fail-first approach, helping students realize what they think they know, but do not.

While the pros far outweigh the cons, there are still a few challenges with this approach:

  • Success is difficult to measure (inherent with AI, not just the class structure).
  • Students who are averse to failure may try to perfect their work, creating a heavy load.
  • A fail-first environment for an ambiguous topic can hurt motivation.

The following sections explain why the fail-first approach is important, as well as a framework for implementation and for overcoming its challenges.

Avoiding Failure: The Practice Trap and Antifragility

The practice trap is a common psychological pitfall where a person spends excessive time “preparing” or “learning” to avoid the discomfort of actually doing the work and experiencing failure. It feels like progress, but it is often a form of sophisticated procrastination.

The result is passive learning and high cognitive load.

This is expected to be common as students embrace seemingly large projects with ambiguous goals (inherent in agentic AI solutions), and especially for coding backgrounds where goals and tests are more specific and concrete.

How to Break Out

  • The 2:1 Rule: For every hour of consuming information, spend two hours producing or practicing.
  • Iterative Shipping: Build a “bad” version of your goal immediately to get real-world feedback.
  • Deliberate Practice: Focus specifically on the skills that make you feel frustrated or incompetent, rather than the ones that feel good.

The practice of agent engineering itself is deeply rooted in iterative shipping. It is common practice in industry settings to ship a “bad” product, observe behavior, and refine it. This leads into the core of how this project-centric curriculum is delivered.

A brief caution: It is possible for professors to be tempted to break down these projects too much, causing more students to complete their coursework, but fewer students to be prepared for similar projects outside of class when the scaffolding is removed. The scaffolding lies greatly in building concrete skills and creating strong in-class support as they embrace difficulty. If it is done well in the beginning of the semester, the end of semester will be far less stressful for students.

This practice of embracing difficulty to become stronger is called antifragility, a term coined by Nassim Nicholas Taleb, but made relevant in this context by Jonathan Haidt.

Content Structure

Project 1: Basics

Create a powerful, unpredictable, “bad” agent

Early on, students are given the most current tools that allow them to make powerful agents with vast capabilities, only to realize those agents are unpredictable and hard to control. Ideally, what they make is cool but not useful. This creates a clear foundation for why diagnostics and context engineering are so important.

Project 2: Diagnostics

Know “why” it does not work and what it should be doing

Next is defining how an agent should behave, measuring it, and observing it to see whether it behaves correctly. This is an essential prerequisite to making a good agent. It is not an end state.

Project 3: Context Engineering

Make a “good” agent

Equipped with diagnostics, students can use context engineering with deliberate intent and utilization of agent/workflow structures to influence the agent to be reliable, predictable, consistent, and safe.

Capstone

These last two projects are dual-purposed as personal learning tools and to make the course living and self-sustaining.

Contribution Assignment

In class experience during this time shifts. While students begin independent projects, classes consist of ethics discussions, brief student-taught lessons, Q&A, or hot topics discussions, followed by work time. The instructor visits with students to provide direction and useful suggestions.

  • “What can I do with AI?”

Maintaining Motivation

Fear of failure is not the only inhibitor to motivation. Agentic AI is inherently ambiguous, and even experts in industry often do not have solid measures of success.

Antidotes include:

  • Finding success, often in small wins
  • Belonging
  • Reduced cognitive load (knowing what is expected)
  • Curiosity

Finding Success

Agent creation is ambiguous, and success may not come to the extent students would like.

Solutions:

  • Peer feedback
  • Measuring improvement and effort
  • Seeing successful examples

Every class begins with a discussion first within groups, then between groups and the class. Topics include:

  • What unresolved challenges did they encounter?
  • What successes did they have, including challenges overcome?
  • Who had the best result and how did they achieve it?
  • What did they learn generally (particularly when there was a reading before class)?

In-class conversation after projects and assignments is essential to understanding whether sufficient progress has been made and what is missing. This structure is especially useful following a major project, where some may feel lost because they did not accomplish what they hoped to, but they have identified learning gaps. They can ask questions and see stronger examples.

For some projects or assignments, the instructor may also provide an expert example for analysis.

Ultimately, students should report less on arbitrary goals and more on improvement, matching the development lifecycle of agentic AI solutions.

To improve class discussions, students should receive prompts that help them self-assess and a framework for writing and identifying their own questions.

Students should also have an easy way to request feedback from the professor through Canvas if needed.

Belonging: Group Work

Students are placed in groups of four (ideally) based on a mix of prior ability (one of the strongest predictors of success according to research). This creates two major benefits:

  1. It follows a “no one left behind” principle by mixing stronger and less experienced students.
  2. Active groups increase belonging and provide more opportunities for peer teaching.

Most work is self-directed and group-supported, but the productization project may be group-led.

Group Formation Process

  1. Students place themselves on a two-axis graph of prior ability with coding (x) and AI fluency (y).
  2. Students in the bottom-left quadrant pick someone in the top-right quadrant.
  3. Students place themselves on a second graph of comfort with ambiguity (x) and interpersonal or leadership ability (y).
  4. Students in the bottom-left quadrant pick someone in the top-right quadrant to join their team, creating teams of three.
  5. Whoever is left chooses a team using both charts.

Teams should sit so they can see one another’s computers and discuss easily.

Cognitive Load

High cognitive load decreases a student’s ability to learn and increases stress. Every structural element outlined in this document is intended to reduce cognitive load.

Additional reductions:

  • Grading is spelled out clearly at the start of the semester.
  • Assignments are clearly marked in Canvas (even if hosted on the website or not requiring submission).
  • Work follows a consistent pattern.

Course Structure: Consistent Pattern

Every Two Weeks

  • A unit project is due.
  • In off weeks, a simple project check-in assignment is due.

Every Week

  • Reading is completed before the first day of class.
  • Assignment is completed before the second day of class.

Every Class

  • Group discussion:
    • Students first report to their group and grade one another on preparation.
    • Each group nominates an insightful idea, comment, or successful method.
  • Class discussion (variable length)
  • Lecture and activities
  • Optional work time

End of Semester

  • Personal self-assessment
  • Group self-assessment
  • Coding interview test (make an agent, fix a bug, create a UI)
  • Final letter

Curiosity

Curiosity is an inherent internal motivator that strongly influences positive learning outcomes according to established research.

Because this is a niche course and not part of a general curriculum, students are assumed to be internally motivated by genuine interest. Given room to explore, most students will do so.

The biggest external force that may drive enrollment is job market pressure: belief that these skills are required for future opportunity.

To foster curiosity, some assignments use a time requirement rather than an accomplishment requirement, including the final project and contribution assignment. Smaller assignments or readings may include a general guideline, resources, or an “A, B, C, or other” path. Students should be able to test prompts and generate many ideas.

Curiosity must be balanced against decision paralysis, which is a real risk in a broad field with ambiguous goals.

Depth and Breadth

Because AI agent programming is broad, it is possible to teach many topics shallowly instead of a few deeply.

The main body of the course teaches fundamental concepts and helps students identify use cases and interests. With those goals in mind, students can pursue breadth topics that matter most to them in the final weeks.

Using OpenAI’s internal framing, the curriculum is T-shaped:

  • The course emphasizes depth (the vertical bar).
  • Students discover breadth (the top bar) from that strong base.

Breadth can be learned through in-class Q and A, student-taught lessons, or self-directed exploration as part of final projects. In final weeks, the professor’s most important role is to equip students with resources, not prescribe every action.

Grading and Assessment

The goal of a course is generally learning. BYU-Idaho’s mission also includes becoming disciple-leaders, so principles of character and discipleship are included in coursework and grading.

As a general rule, accomplishment in this course is based on:

  • Basic ability (binary outcomes like “code an agent that does…”)
  • Depth of learning (non-binary)
  • Improvement or effort
  • Leadership or helpfulness (did others in class grow because of you?)

These assessments should stay visible throughout the semester as feedback mechanisms. Feedback is more influential than the threat of a bad grade.

Basic Ability

  • A timed code interview assessment
  • Mostly core must-have skills, with a few stretch questions
  • An A requires completing all “must” questions
  • Open note, likely open AI

Completion of major projects may include an arbitrary quality cutoff.

Readings

If students are in this class, they can use AI easily. They can use AI to cheat, or more likely to skim readings and submit surface-level work.

Therefore, the focus is not on completion alone, but on whether learning occurred and how well.

Instead of grading a reading submission directly, each reading assignment is graded by:

  • An honor submission (“I am prepared for class discussion”)
  • Optional self-rating scale (for example, 1 to 5)
  • Peer judgment of preparation based on beginning-of-class group discussion
  • Potential tally of meaningful comments

Peer judgments are not as strong as self-judgments but support accountability. They should be used primarily as feedback and as input for the final letter.

Students can make up for an absence by speaking with a peer on another day or submitting writing beforehand showing what they learned. Either option receives partial points.

Assignments

Because assignment accomplishment alone does not measure true growth, students provide:

  • Proof of work (screenshot, codebase, file)
  • Explanation of what they accomplished
  • Self-report of time spent consuming vs producing

Assignments should include time limits to reduce cognitive load (for example, “You are only allowed to spend x minutes on this assignment”). More time should not imply better learning or a higher grade.

Unrelated to grade, students should have an easy Canvas pathway to request professor feedback if they want it.

Character

Character is difficult to measure directly, so it is assessed through guided survey instruments:

  • Self-assessments for improvement and effort
  • Peer assessments for leadership and helpfulness

Example prompts:

  • I have overcome a learning hurdle or solved a problem because of this person.
  • This person has been a positive influence for me in this class.
  • For a __ credit class, I put in a sufficient amount of time and effort.
  • I have improved the following skills (with sliders for given skills).

One final project is a moral manifesto.

True Grade Rubric

How are these measures weighted in a final grade? In short:

  • A: Did your best work
  • B: Did the work
  • C: Did some work
  • D: Did some stuff
  • F: Did nothing

Students decide this in a final letter, and the instructor approves it.

Final Letter

Although the final grade may come down to “Did you do your best work,” students may be unsure how to assess that. To support self-assessment and honest accountability, students submit a final letter with:

  • Statement of accomplishment
  • Self-assessment
  • Self-reflection and future plans
  • Grade decision

Statement of Accomplishment Includes

  • Number of projects completed/submitted
  • Time spent on the self-directed final project and contribution
  • Whether they delivered a contribution
  • Score on coding interview
  • Score on character survey assessments (self and peer)

Self-Reflection May Include

  • Have you met the course outcomes?
  • Work you are most proud of
  • Challenges experienced and how you overcame them
  • Report on each project and skills developed
  • What role Christ played in helping you overcome challenges
  • Plans for continued skill development or community contribution
  • Plans for improving character in future classes or life work
  • Expressions of gratitude toward God, classmates, or instructor
  • Suggestions for course improvements

Finally, students report their grade and final reasons for it. They should have freedom to assess which measures are most important to them.

To reduce writing load, an example and structured template may be provided. To improve memory and reflection quality, smaller letters can be submitted at the end of each unit and later merged (including with AI support) before final comments are added.

Rubric Snapshot (For Students Who Need More Structure)

Category A B C D
Main Projects (1-4) Completed all 4, best work Completed all 4, minimal work Completed 2-3 Incomplete progress
Self-Directed Project Put in __ hours and improved skills substantially or learned important lessons Put in __ hours with meaningful effort Put in __ hours with limited growth Minimal engagement
Contribution Assignment Made a meaningful contribution Made contribution with minimal effort Made no contribution No attempt
Code Interview (with retry) 3-4 2 1 0
Self-Assessment __ points __ points __ points __ points
Peer-Assessment __ points __ points __ points __ points

Only reveal the full rubric when students are anxious or request greater structure. For everyone else, reveal the assessment measures they will use for self-evaluation.

The rubric gives the professor an objective anchor for evaluating grades while still leaving room for mercy.
